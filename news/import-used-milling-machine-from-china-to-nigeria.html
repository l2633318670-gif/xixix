How to Import a Used Milling Machine from China to Nigeria

Importing a used milling machine from China to Nigeria is straightforward once you understand the three things that matter most: shipping, documents, and customs rules. This guide walks through the full process so you can plan your budget and avoid delays at the port. The same steps apply to Kenya, Ghana, Tanzania, and most other African destinations.

1. Choose the machine and get it on video

Start by confirming the exact machine, condition, and price. Always ask for a full video of the machine running before you pay — this is the single best way to avoid disputes. See our used milling machine inspection checklist for what to check.

2. Understand shipping time and cost

Heavy machines almost always ship by sea freight, which is the cheapest option. As a rough guide, sea freight from China to West African ports such as Lagos (Apapa / Tin Can) takes about 25–40 days, depending on the route and transshipment.

  • FCL (Full Container Load): best when the machine fills a 20ft or 40ft container, or you buy several machines.
  • Breakbulk / RoRo: sometimes used for very large single machines that do not fit a standard container.

3. Prepare the right documents

Most shipments to Nigeria need this core set of documents. Missing paperwork is the number-one cause of customs delays:

  • Commercial Invoice — shows the machine, price, and buyer/seller details.
  • Packing List — lists what is in the shipment, weights, and dimensions.
  • Bill of Lading (B/L) — proof the cargo is on the ship.
  • Certificate of Origin — confirms the goods come from China.
  • Fumigation Certificate — required when wooden pallets or crates are used.
  • Cargo Tracking Note (ECTN / CTN) — required by many African countries, including Nigeria, before the cargo arrives.

4. Check import permits and used-machine rules

Used (second-hand) machinery is treated differently from new goods in many countries. Before you buy, confirm with a local customs broker:

  • Whether an import permit is needed for the machine.
  • Any age limit on used machinery (some countries restrict machines over a certain age).
  • Applicable import duty and VAT so you can budget the landed cost.

5. Inspect before shipping, use a local broker

A short video and clear photos before payment, plus loading and container-seal photos before departure, make clearance far smoother. On the destination side, a local customs broker who knows your port will save time and money.

Typical step-by-step timeline

  • Day 0 — Confirm machine + video, agree price (machine + sea freight to your port).
  • Day 1–7 — Payment, cleaning, rust protection, wooden packing, loading photos.
  • Week 1 — Export documents prepared and container booked.
  • Week 4–6 — Arrival at port; broker clears with the documents above.
